How to maximize your credit card rewards in Canada: the data your statements are hiding

Most Canadians with two or more credit cards leave $400–$800 a year on the table — not because they chose the wrong cards, but because they use the wrong one at checkout. I built a statement analyzer to prove it. Here's what the data shows.

By Deep Bando · June 2026 · 12 min read · Credit Cards
Why

I spent a decade managing credit card portfolios worth more than $20 billion. I watched how the economics worked from the inside: interchange fees, rewards liability, category earn rates, partner funding agreements. I knew exactly how much each card was worth in each spending category, for each cardholder type.

What I didn't know until recently was how badly I was managing my own cards.

I have four cards in my wallet — a CIBC Costco Mastercard, a TD Infinite First Class Visa, an Amex Cobalt, and a Wealthsimple Cash Card. Each one earns meaningfully more in specific categories. The Amex earns 5x on food. The TD earns premium travel points on everything. The CIBC earns 3% cash back at Costco gas and restaurants. I knew all of this, and I still used them almost at random.

So I did what I'd do at work: I pulled the data. I built a statement analyzer that converts every PDF statement into structured transaction data, cleans merchant names, maps every purchase to a category, and computes what I actually earned versus what I could have earned if I'd used the optimal card every time.

"The analysis showed I was leaving $612 a year in unrealized rewards — not because my card selection was wrong, but because my card selection at the moment of checkout was wrong."

The point of this essay is not to tell you which card to get. It's to show you what your own statement data actually reveals — and how to use that insight to stop giving your rewards to merchants you never intended to subsidize.

What

The misallocation problem hiding in your wallet

Here is the core problem. You have multiple cards. Each card earns at different rates in different categories. Your wallet contains the full optimization surface — the right answer is already in your pocket. But at the moment of payment, you reach for whatever card is on top, or whatever you remember, or whatever you used last time.

Banks design reward programs to earn your volume, not to make the optimal choice obvious. The earn-rate tables are buried in 40-page benefit guides. The category definitions are deliberately fuzzy. And no card issuer in Canada is going to remind you at checkout that today's purchase would earn three times more on the other card in your wallet.

The information asymmetry is structural. And the cost of it is measurable.

How

The three layers where money leaks

The month-over-month total is almost always a distraction. The real story is in what moved and why. I've found the analysis naturally decomposes into three layers, each revealing a different type of error.

Layer 1: Category shift

The first question is whether a category got more expensive, or just moved to a different card. These look identical in the total, but they require completely different responses. In May, my dining spend actually dropped — but it dropped on the Amex (5x) and reappeared on the TD (2x). Net result: same dollars spent, materially fewer points earned.

Layer 2: Merchant drift

Category analysis shows you what. Merchant analysis shows you where specifically — and where the fix lives. In my case, the dining increase wasn't restaurant inflation. It was a new habit: two new delivery merchants, both tagged incorrectly as "Shopping" by TD's statement, which meant I wasn't even using my Amex for them because I didn't realize they were food.

Merchant name cleaning is where most DIY statement analysis breaks down. Credit card statements print merchant names as they appear in the payment network — truncated, code-suffixed, inconsistently formatted. "TIM HOR #1241 ON" and "TIMHORTONS TORONTO" are the same merchant. Without normalization, your category totals are garbage.

This is the exhibit that changes behaviour. Not the spend totals. Not the categories. The explicit calculation of what you actually earned versus what you could have earned — by merchant, by month, annualized.

When you see it as a number, the abstraction collapses. It stops being "I should use my Amex more for food" and becomes "I left $612 in rewards on the table in the last 12 months." That's a flight. That's a hotel night. That's real money that was already authorized and already spent — you just didn't collect it.

What If

The system, not the habit

Here is what I've learned from a decade of building credit card decision systems professionally, and then applying the same thinking to my own wallet personally: the problem is not knowledge. You probably already know your Amex is better for dining. The problem is execution at the moment of payment — a three-second window with no information in front of you and no friction to reaching for the wrong card.

Banks solved this problem for themselves years ago. Every transaction you make is run through a decision engine that routes it correctly in milliseconds — to the right bureau, the right risk model, the right fraud check. The machine makes the decision perfectly every time because the decision is made before the human is involved.

You need the same thing for your side of the transaction.

"The analysis doesn't change your behaviour at the point of payment. What changes behaviour is having the answer already computed — so the decision at checkout is a one-second lookup, not a three-card mental calculation."

The statement dashboard I built solves the awareness problem. It shows you the data, the patterns, the opportunity cost. It makes the invisible visible. But awareness without a decision tool is incomplete — you'll look at the data, feel motivated for a week, and then go back to reaching for the top card in your wallet.

The final piece is a tool that sits at the point of decision: your wallet loaded, your spend context understood, the right card surfaced automatically before you tap your phone. That's the problem SuperCards is built to solve.

Common questions

How much money am I leaving on the table with multiple credit cards?

Most Canadians with two or more credit cards leave $400–$800 per year in unrealized rewards by routinely using a lower-earning card at checkout. The exact number depends on your wallet composition and total monthly spend, but the analysis above shows how to calculate it precisely from your own statements.

What is the best credit card combination for Canadians in 2026?

There is no single best combination — it depends on your spend mix. The most effective Canadian wallet typically combines a high-earn dining and grocery card (Amex Cobalt at 5x food is the current leader), a strong general-spend or travel card (TD Infinite First Class Visa), and a no-fee cash-back card for categories not covered by the others. The key is not which cards you have — it's whether you use them correctly at checkout.

How do I analyze my credit card statements in Canada?

The most effective approach is to convert your PDF statements to structured data (tools like markitdown work well), normalize merchant names to remove location codes and formatting noise, map every transaction to a spend category, and then compute actual earned versus potential earned by card. The CLAUDE.md file and analyzer described in this essay automates this workflow end-to-end.

Is the Amex Cobalt worth it for groceries and dining in Canada?

At 5x Membership Rewards points on food and drink (eligible grocery, restaurant, food delivery, and bar spend), the Amex Cobalt has the highest earn rate of any Canadian card in those categories. At a conservative redemption value of $0.01 per point, that's an effective 5% return on a category that represents 30–40% of most households' discretionary spend. The annual fee is $155.88. For most multi-card Canadians, it pays for itself within two to three months of correctly routing dining and grocery spend to it.
